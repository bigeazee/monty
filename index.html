i What is Monty and how does it work? ▾

What Monty does

Given a list of work items, each with a rough size and an uncertainty score, Monty predicts a range of plausible end dates for the whole programme - and for each release within it. Instead of giving you a single date that's almost certainly wrong, you get a confidence range you can plan against.

Why a range, not a single date?

Real work doesn't take exactly the time you guess. Some tasks finish faster than expected, more take longer (the "optimism bias" backed by decades of project research). If you ask the team for a single estimate and commit to it, you'll be late roughly half the time. A range tells you: here's the date we'd hit on a normal day, here's how late it could realistically slip.

What's a Monte Carlo simulation?

Named after the casino - and where Monty gets his name. The idea: instead of doing the maths by hand, you run thousands of imaginary versions of the project. In each version, every item gets a slightly different duration sampled from its plausible range. Monty schedules them, records when the project ends, and repeats - 1,000 runs by default.

Then he sorts all those end dates from earliest to latest and sees how they cluster. That cluster is the forecast.

P50, P80, P90 - what do they mean?

These are percentiles. After all the simulated end dates have been sorted from earliest to latest:

  • P50 is the date 50% of simulations finish by. Your "coin flip" date - even chance of beating it.
  • P80 is the date 80% finish by. A reasonable commitment date for stakeholders.
  • P90 is the date 90% finish by. The "things would have to go quite wrong to miss this" date.

The gap between P50 and P90 is your schedule risk. Wide gap = lots of uncertainty; narrow gap = tight prediction.

How estimates become durations

Each item gets a T-shirt size (XS to XL), an uncertainty level (Low/Medium/High), and a number of people assigned to it. The Duration model produces a distribution of effort in person-weeks: the size's base value sets the mode, and the uncertainty profile multipliers stretch it into a (min, mode, max) range.

Each simulation samples a value from that distribution and applies the parallelism efficiency to get the item's duration. So an L item (5 person-week base = 25 person-days) with 2 people takes ~15 working days at the default α = 0.75 - about 40% faster than one person doing it alone over 25 days. All forecast outputs are in working days, automatically excluding weekends and bank holidays.

Parallelism follows a power law: duration = effort / peopleα. With the default α = 0.75, two people deliver ~40% faster than one (not 50%); four people ~65% faster (not 75%). This captures the coordination overhead Brooks's law warns about, without making the model non-monotonic. Tune α in the Duration model section if your team has different empirical data.

Triangular vs Uniform - which to use?

Triangular by default. It uses all three numbers (min, mode, max), peaks at the mode, and tapers toward both edges - so the asymmetric "longer right tail" the duration model encodes is preserved in the forecast.

Uniform treats every value between min and max as equally likely; the mode is ignored. It's the "I genuinely have no idea where typical lies" model - useful as a worst-case sanity check, or for very early sizing where the team can't yet anchor on a most-likely value.

If you switch to Uniform, also switch the Profile preset to "Symmetric" - Monty will warn you when they're inconsistent. There's no widely-agreed industry-standard symmetric profile for software (because real data skews right), so the symmetric values are derived as a pragmatic equal-spread version of the asymmetric defaults.

How items get scheduled

Monty maintains a pool of headcount people. Items are picked up in the order shown in the items table (drag rows to reprioritise); each one consumes its assigned people from the pool. When the pool runs out, no new items can start until a running item finishes and frees its people back. This means concurrency varies - if you have 4 people and three 1-person items, three run in parallel; if the next item needs 4 people, it has to wait for everything to finish.

This is repeated for every simulation run, so predecessor variance flows through naturally - if an early item runs long, everything queued behind it shifts.

How to read the output

The headline cards show the overall P50/P80/P90 dates and total effort in person-weeks. The release cards break this down per version. The Gantt chart shows each item's P50 schedule with a P10-P90 spread bar; release milestones at the bottom mark the slip range per version. The distribution chart shows the shape of all simulated end dates - if it has a long right tail, that's a sign of tail risk worth thinking about.

A health warning

Monty's forecast is only as good as the inputs. Garbage T-shirt sizes give garbage outputs. The model assumes items are independent - it doesn't know about hidden dependencies, key-person risk, or that QA always takes longer in December. Treat the dates as a conversation starter, not a commitment. Re-run the forecast as estimates firm up; the spread should narrow as you learn more.
